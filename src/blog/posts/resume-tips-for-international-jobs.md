      <h2>1. Understand the Difference: Resume vs. CV vs. Lebenslauf</h2>
      <p>Before you start writing, know what the employer expects to receive:</p>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Resume</strong> (US, Canada, Australia) — A concise 1–2 page document focused on achievements and skills. No photo, no date of birth, no marital status.</li>
        <li><strong>CV / Curriculum Vitae</strong> (UK, Ireland, most of Europe) — Typically 2 pages, sometimes longer in academic contexts. May include personal details depending on the country.</li>
        <li><strong>Lebenslauf</strong> (Germany, Austria, Switzerland) — A structured, chronological CV that traditionally includes a professional photo, date of birth, and nationality. Usually 2–3 pages.</li>
        <li><strong>Europass CV</strong> (EU-wide) — A standardised CV format recognised across the European Union. Useful as a starting point, but many employers in competitive markets prefer a more polished format.</li>
      </ul>

      <!-- ==================== SECTION 2 ==================== -->
      <h2>2. Country-Specific Resume Formats</h2>
      <p>Each country has its own unwritten rules. Here is what employers expect in the most popular relocation destinations.</p>

      <h3>🇩🇪 Germany</h3>
      <p>German employers expect a very specific format. Getting this wrong signals that you haven't done your research.</p>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-4">
        <li><strong>Photo:</strong> A professional headshot in the top-right corner is standard (and still widely expected despite anti-discrimination law)</li>
        <li><strong>Personal details:</strong> Include date of birth, nationality, and sometimes marital status</li>
        <li><strong>Structure:</strong> Strictly reverse-chronological. No gaps — if you took time off, explain it</li>
        <li><strong>Length:</strong> 2–3 pages. Academic qualifications are valued highly</li>
        <li><strong>Certificates:</strong> Attach copies of degrees, employment references (<em>Arbeitszeugnisse</em>), and language certificates</li>
        <li><strong>Language:</strong> Write in English if the job posting is in English; otherwise write in German</li>
      </ul>

      <div class="bg-yellow-50 dark:bg-yellow-900/20 border-l-4 border-yellow-400 dark:border-yellow-600 p-4 rounded-r-lg mb-6">
        <p class="text-sm text-gray-700 dark:text-gray-300 mb-0"><strong>Tip:</strong> German employers take <em>Arbeitszeugnisse</em> (employment references) seriously. These are coded letters written by your previous employer. If you don't have them, prepare a brief explanation. Read more in our <a href="/resources/country-guides/germany/" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline">Germany relocation guide</a>.</p>
      </div>

      <p>Useful tools for German CVs:</p>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><a href="https://europa.eu/europass/en/create-europass-cv" target="_blank" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">Europass CV Creator</a> — Free EU-standard CV builder, accepted across Germany</li>

      </ul>

      <h3>🇬🇧 United Kingdom</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-4">
        <li><strong>Photo:</strong> Never include a photo — it's considered unprofessional and potentially discriminatory</li>
        <li><strong>Personal details:</strong> Do not include date of birth, marital status, or nationality</li>
        <li><strong>Structure:</strong> 2 pages maximum. Lead with a personal statement, then experience (reverse-chronological), then education</li>
        <li><strong>Tone:</strong> Achievement-focused with quantified results</li>
        <li><strong>References:</strong> "References available upon request" is sufficient — don't list them</li>
      </ul>
      <p>See our <a href="/resources/country-guides/uk/" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline">UK relocation guide</a> for more on working in the United Kingdom.</p>

      <h3>🇳🇱 Netherlands</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-4">
        <li><strong>Photo:</strong> Optional but common</li>
        <li><strong>Style:</strong> Concise and direct — Dutch employers value clarity over flourish</li>
        <li><strong>Length:</strong> 1–2 pages</li>
        <li><strong>Soft skills:</strong> Emphasise teamwork, communication, and cultural adaptability more than in other countries</li>
        <li><strong>Language:</strong> Always in English for international roles</li>
      </ul>
      <p>See our <a href="/resources/country-guides/netherlands/" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline">Netherlands relocation guide</a> for visa and cost-of-living details.</p>

      <h3>🇮🇪 Ireland</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-4">
        <li><strong>Photo:</strong> Not expected</li>
        <li><strong>Structure:</strong> Similar to the UK — 2 pages, achievement-based, reverse-chronological</li>
        <li><strong>Language:</strong> English</li>
        <li><strong>Tech sector:</strong> For tech multinationals (Google, Meta, Apple), follow the US resume style — concise, metrics-driven</li>
      </ul>
      <p>See our <a href="/resources/country-guides/ireland/" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline">Ireland relocation guide</a>.</p>

      <h3>🇸🇪 Sweden</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-4">
        <li><strong>Photo:</strong> Optional</li>
        <li><strong>Structure:</strong> 1–2 pages, reverse-chronological</li>
        <li><strong>Tone:</strong> Straightforward and humble — avoid superlatives. Swedish work culture values <em>lagom</em> (moderation)</li>
        <li><strong>Personal number:</strong> Not required at application stage, but you will need a <em>personnummer</em> once employed</li>
      </ul>
      <p>See our <a href="/resources/country-guides/sweden/" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline">Sweden relocation guide</a>.</p>

      <h3>🇨🇭 Switzerland</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-4">
        <li><strong>Photo:</strong> Expected — professional quality</li>
        <li><strong>Personal details:</strong> Include date of birth, nationality, and work permit status</li>
        <li><strong>Language:</strong> Match the language of the job posting. Switzerland has German, French, Italian, and English-speaking work environments depending on the region</li>
        <li><strong>Length:</strong> 2 pages, highly structured</li>
        <li><strong>References:</strong> List 2–3 professional references with contact details</li>
      </ul>
      <p>See our <a href="/resources/country-guides/switzerland/" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline">Switzerland relocation guide</a>.</p>

      <h3>🇨🇦 Canada</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-4">
        <li><strong>Photo:</strong> Never — it's against hiring regulations</li>
        <li><strong>Personal details:</strong> No age, gender, marital status, or religion</li>
        <li><strong>Structure:</strong> Skills-based or combination format, 1–2 pages</li>
        <li><strong>Keyword optimisation:</strong> Canadian employers use ATS heavily — mirror the job posting language</li>
      </ul>
      <p>See our <a href="/resources/country-guides/canada/" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline">Canada relocation guide</a>.</p>

      <h3>🇦🇺 Australia</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-4">
        <li><strong>Photo:</strong> Not expected</li>
        <li><strong>Structure:</strong> 2–4 pages are acceptable (longer than US/UK norms)</li>
        <li><strong>Key information:</strong> Include visa status and right to work early in the document</li>
        <li><strong>References:</strong> Provide 2–3 references with full contact details</li>
      </ul>
      <p>See our <a href="/resources/country-guides/australia/" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline">Australia relocation guide</a>.</p>

      <h2>4. How to Beat Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS)</h2>
      <p>Most large companies and recruitment agencies use Applicant Tracking Systems to filter resumes before a human ever sees them. Studies suggest that up to 75% of resumes are rejected by ATS before reaching a recruiter<sup><a href="#fn1">[1]</a></sup>. Here is how to make sure yours gets through.</p>

      <h3>ATS basics</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Use standard section headers:</strong> "Work Experience", "Education", "Skills" — not creative alternatives like "My Journey" or "Toolkit"</li>
        <li><strong>Avoid tables, columns, and graphics:</strong> Most ATS cannot parse multi-column layouts or embedded images</li>
        <li><strong>Use a simple file format:</strong> PDF is usually safe. Some older ATS prefer .docx — check the job posting</li>
        <li><strong>Mirror keywords from the job posting:</strong> If the posting says "React.js", don't write "ReactJS". If it says "project management", don't write "PM"</li>
        <li><strong>Include exact job title variations:</strong> "Software Engineer" and "Software Developer" may be indexed differently</li>
        <li><strong>Don't put important information in headers/footers:</strong> Many ATS skip header/footer content entirely</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>ATS checking tools</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><a href="https://www.jobscan.co/" target="_blank" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">Jobscan</a> — Compares your resume against a specific job description and gives a match score with specific improvement suggestions</li>
        <li><a href="https://resumeworded.com/" target="_blank" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">Resume Worded</a> — AI-powered resume scorer that checks ATS compatibility and gives line-by-line feedback</li>
        <li><a href="https://skillsyncer.com/" target="_blank" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">SkillSyncer</a> — Keyword matching tool that highlights missing hard and soft skills from the job posting</li>
      </ul>

      <div class="bg-blue-50 dark:bg-blue-900/20 border-l-4 border-blue-400 dark:border-blue-600 p-4 rounded-r-lg mb-6">
        <p class="text-sm text-gray-700 dark:text-gray-300 mb-0"><strong>Pro tip:</strong> Create a "master resume" with all your experience, then create tailored versions for each application. The effort of customising your resume for each job posting makes a significant difference in callback rates.</p>
      </div>

      <h2>5. Writing Effective Cover Letters for International Jobs</h2>
      <p>Cover letter expectations vary dramatically by country. In some places they are mandatory; in others they are a waste of time.</p>

      <h3>Country-by-country expectations</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Germany:</strong> Cover letters (<em>Anschreiben</em>) are expected and taken seriously. Address a specific person, explain why you want to work at <em>this</em> company in <em>this</em> city, and explain any career gaps. Keep it to one page</li>
        <li><strong>UK:</strong> Expected but can be brief. Focus on why you are a good fit for the specific role. 3–4 paragraphs</li>
        <li><strong>Netherlands:</strong> A short motivation letter is expected. Be direct and practical — Dutch culture values straightforwardness</li>
        <li><strong>US/Canada:</strong> Often optional. When included, keep it to half a page. Some companies explicitly ask you not to include one</li>
        <li><strong>Sweden:</strong> A personal letter (<em>personligt brev</em>) is expected. Focus on cultural fit and why you want to live and work in Sweden</li>
        <li><strong>Ireland:</strong> Brief cover letter expected for most roles. For tech multinationals, follow the US approach</li>
        <li><strong>Switzerland:</strong> Expected and should be thorough. Address it in the language of the job posting</li>
      </ul>

      <div class="bg-yellow-50 dark:bg-yellow-900/20 border-l-4 border-yellow-400 dark:border-yellow-600 p-4 rounded-r-lg mb-6">
        <p class="text-sm text-gray-700 dark:text-gray-300 mb-0"><strong>Important:</strong> In your cover letter, always address the visa question proactively. If you need sponsorship, say so clearly and explain why you are worth the investment. If you already have the right to work, state that upfront — it removes a major concern for the employer.</p>
      </div>

      <!-- ==================== SECTION 6 ==================== -->
      <h2>6. Quantify Your Impact</h2>
      <p>International employers receiving hundreds of applications from around the world need concrete evidence of your value. Companies sponsoring visas face additional costs — legal fees, relocation packages, onboarding delays — so they need to be convinced that hiring you specifically is worth the extra investment.</p>

      <h3>How to quantify effectively</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Revenue impact:</strong> "Increased conversion rate by 23%, generating an estimated $150K in additional annual revenue"</li>
        <li><strong>Scale:</strong> "Built and maintained a microservices architecture serving 2M daily active users"</li>
        <li><strong>Efficiency:</strong> "Reduced CI/CD pipeline time from 45 minutes to 12 minutes, saving 20 engineering hours per week"</li>
        <li><strong>Team leadership:</strong> "Led a cross-functional team of 8 engineers across 3 time zones to deliver a product redesign 2 weeks ahead of schedule"</li>
        <li><strong>Cost savings:</strong> "Migrated infrastructure to Kubernetes, reducing cloud costs by 35% ($200K annually)"</li>
      </ul>
      <p>If you don't have exact numbers, estimate conservatively and frame it clearly: "approximately", "an estimated", "around". Some quantification is always better than none.</p>

      <!-- ==================== SECTION 7 ==================== -->
      <h2>7. Lead with Your International Readiness</h2>
      <p>Place your willingness to relocate prominently in your resume summary. Employers want to know you are serious about moving and won't back out after receiving an offer.</p>

      <h3>What to include</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Relocation statement:</strong> "Open to immediate relocation to Berlin / willing to relocate to the Netherlands"</li>
        <li><strong>Visa status:</strong> "Eligible for EU Blue Card" or "UK Skilled Worker visa sponsorship required"</li>
        <li><strong>Language skills:</strong> Use the <a href="https://www.coe.int/en/web/common-european-framework-reference-languages/level-descriptions" target="_blank" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">CEFR scale</a> (A1–C2) for European applications: "German — B2, French — B1, English — C2"</li>
        <li><strong>International experience:</strong> Previous work abroad, study abroad, global clients, cross-cultural team experience</li>
        <li><strong>Timezone flexibility:</strong> If relevant, mention experience working across time zones</li>
      </ul>

      <div class="bg-blue-50 dark:bg-blue-900/20 border-l-4 border-blue-400 dark:border-blue-600 p-4 rounded-r-lg mb-6">
        <p class="text-sm text-gray-700 dark:text-gray-300 mb-0"><strong>Note:</strong> Be specific about visa eligibility. Saying "I need sponsorship" is less helpful than "I qualify for the EU Blue Card based on my degree and the salary offered." Research the specific visa programme you would use — our <a href="/resources/visa-information/" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline">visa information page</a> can help.</p>

      <!-- ==================== SECTION 8 ==================== -->
      <h2>8. Optimise Your LinkedIn Profile for International Recruiters</h2>
      <p>LinkedIn is the primary tool recruiters use to find international candidates. According to LinkedIn's own data, users with an "Open to Work" location preference outside their home country receive significantly more recruiter messages<sup><a href="#fn2">[2]</a></sup>.</p>

      <h3>Profile optimisation checklist</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Headline:</strong> Include your target location. Example: "Senior Backend Engineer | Open to relocation to Germany / Netherlands"</li>
        <li><strong>Open to Work settings:</strong> Go to your profile → "Open to" → "Finding a new job" → Set your preferred locations to your target countries/cities</li>
        <li><strong>Location:</strong> If you are currently abroad, some candidates set their LinkedIn location to their target city to appear in local searches. Be upfront about your actual location in conversations</li>
        <li><strong>About section:</strong> Mention relocation willingness, visa eligibility, and language skills in the first 2–3 lines (these are visible before the "see more" button)</li>
        <li><strong>Skills section:</strong> Add at least 20 relevant skills — recruiters search by skill keywords. Include country-specific terms (e.g., "Agile Scrum" in Europe, "Scrum Master" in US)</li>
        <li><strong>Language settings:</strong> Add all languages you speak with proficiency levels</li>
        <li><strong>Engage with content:</strong> Comment on posts from companies and people in your target city. LinkedIn's algorithm will start showing your profile to people in that network</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>LinkedIn tools for international job seekers</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/salary/" target="_blank" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">LinkedIn Salary Insights</a> — Research salary ranges by role and city before applying</li>
        <li><a href="https://resumeworded.com/linkedin-review/" target="_blank" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">Resume Worded LinkedIn Review</a> — Free tool that scores your LinkedIn profile and suggests specific improvements</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>9. Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
      <p>These are the errors we see most frequently in applications from international candidates:</p>

      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><i data-lucide="x-circle" class="w-4 h-4 inline text-red-500"></i> <strong>Using the same resume for every country</strong> — A German Lebenslauf and a UK CV are fundamentally different documents</li>
        <li><i data-lucide="x-circle" class="w-4 h-4 inline text-red-500"></i> <strong>Not mentioning visa status</strong> — Recruiters will assume the worst. Be proactive about stating your eligibility</li>
        <li><i data-lucide="x-circle" class="w-4 h-4 inline text-red-500"></i> <strong>Machine-translated resumes</strong> — If you can't write fluently in the local language, apply in English (most international roles accept it)</li>
        <li><i data-lucide="x-circle" class="w-4 h-4 inline text-red-500"></i> <strong>Generic cover letters</strong> — Especially in Germany and Switzerland, a generic cover letter signals low effort</li>
        <li><i data-lucide="x-circle" class="w-4 h-4 inline text-red-500"></i> <strong>Ignoring ATS keywords</strong> — If the job posting says "Kubernetes" and you write "container orchestration", the ATS may not make the connection</li>
        <li><i data-lucide="x-circle" class="w-4 h-4 inline text-red-500"></i> <strong>Missing out on LinkedIn optimisation</strong> — Most international recruiters find candidates through LinkedIn, not job boards</li>
        <li><i data-lucide="x-circle" class="w-4 h-4 inline text-red-500"></i> <strong>Including irrelevant personal information</strong> — For UK/US/Canada applications, do not include age, religion, photo, or marital status</li>
      </ul>
