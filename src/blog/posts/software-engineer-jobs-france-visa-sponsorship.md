      <h2>2. Visa Options for Software Engineers in France</h2>
      <p>France offers several visa pathways for non-EU tech workers. The system has been designed to be fast and transparent — a deliberate strategy to compete with Germany, the Netherlands, and the UK for global talent.</p>

      <h3>Passeport Talent (Talent Passport)</h3>
      <p>This is the primary visa for software engineers relocating to France. It was specifically created to attract skilled workers, startup founders, researchers, and artists.</p>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Eligibility:</strong> A job offer from a French company paying at least 1.5x the minimum annual salary (approximately €39,000 in 2026), plus a bachelor's degree or higher</li>
        <li><strong>Processing time:</strong> As fast as 48 hours through French consulates participating in the fast-track program. Standard processing is 2–4 weeks</li>
        <li><strong>Duration:</strong> Up to 4 years, renewable</li>
        <li><strong>Family:</strong> Your spouse and dependants receive a "Famille de Talent" visa automatically — they can also work in France without restrictions</li>
        <li><strong>Path to permanent residency:</strong> After 5 years of continuous residence</li>
        <li><strong>No employer lock-in:</strong> You can change employers without applying for a new visa</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>EU Blue Card (Carte Bleue Européenne)</h3>
      <p>France also issues EU Blue Cards, which are attractive if you plan to move between EU countries.</p>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Salary threshold:</strong> 1.5x the average gross salary (approximately €54,000 for standard occupations; lower for shortage occupations like IT)</li>
        <li><strong>Duration:</strong> Up to 4 years</li>
        <li><strong>EU mobility:</strong> After 12 months, you can transfer to another EU country with streamlined procedures</li>
        <li><strong>Permanent residency:</strong> After 5 years (time can be split across EU countries)</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Intra-Company Transfer</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>For:</strong> Engineers transferring from a company's non-EU office to its French office</li>
        <li><strong>Requirements:</strong> Minimum 3 months of prior employment with the same company</li>
        <li><strong>Duration:</strong> Up to 3 years</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>6. Do You Need to Speak French?</h2>
      <p>This is the question every engineer asks before considering France. The honest answer: <strong>not for work, but it helps for daily life.</strong></p>

      <p>Most tech companies hiring international engineers operate in English. Companies like Criteo, Datadog, Doctolib, Qonto, and BlaBlaCar have English as their official working language because their engineering teams are international. Your code reviews, standups, documentation, and Slack channels will all be in English.</p>

      <p>However, daily life in France — dealing with your landlord, government offices (préfecture), the doctor, or even ordering at a local bistro — is much easier with basic French. Most international engineers pick up conversational French within 6–12 months, and many companies offer free French classes as part of their relocation package.</p>

        <li><strong>Employer files the visa:</strong> Your employer handles the bulk of the process. For the Passeport Talent, they submit an application to the French labour authority (DIRECCTE). You will need your passport, degree certificates, and the employment contract.</li>
        <li><strong>Visit the French consulate:</strong> Book an appointment at the nearest French consulate. Bring your visa approval, passport photos, proof of accommodation (a hotel booking is fine initially), and travel insurance. In some countries, this step takes 48 hours.</li>
        <li><strong>Arrive and validate your visa:</strong> Within 3 months of arriving, you need to validate your visa online through the ANEF portal and schedule an appointment at the OFII (French immigration office) for a medical check.</li>
        <li><strong>Get settled:</strong> Open a French bank account (Qonto, BoursoBank, or a traditional bank), get a SIM card (Free Mobile is the best value at €19.99/month for unlimited), register for healthcare, and find permanent housing.</li>
      </ol>

      <h2>8. Life in Paris as a Software Engineer</h2>
      <p>Working in Paris as a developer is a fundamentally different experience from Silicon Valley or London — and for most engineers, that is the entire point.</p>

      <p><strong>The 35-hour work week</strong> is real at most French companies. While tech companies tend to be more flexible (and some engineers work more), the culture genuinely respects boundaries. Evenings and weekends are yours. Your manager will not message you at 10 PM, and if they do, France's "right to disconnect" law means you are under no obligation to respond.</p>

      <p><strong>The food.</strong> This is not a trivial point. Paris offers everything from €12 three-course "formule" lunches at neighbourhood bistros to world-class bakeries on every block. Many companies provide "Tickets Restaurant" (meal vouchers worth €8–€12 per day) that are tax-free, effectively subsidising your meals.</p>

      <p><strong>Public transport</strong> in Paris is excellent. The Metro covers the entire city, and a monthly Navigo pass (€86.40, often 50% subsidised by your employer) gives you unlimited metro, bus, RER, and tram access. Most engineers live without a car.</p>

      <p><strong>International community:</strong> Paris has one of Europe's largest communities of international tech workers. Meetups, tech conferences (Devoxx France, dotConferences), and coworking spaces make it easy to build a social network. You will meet engineers from across Europe, North Africa, India, Latin America, and beyond.</p>

      <p><strong>Travel:</strong> Paris is one of Europe's best-connected cities. The Eurostar reaches London in 2 hours 15 minutes. Thalys gets you to Brussels in 1.5 hours, Amsterdam in 3.5 hours. Budget airlines fly everywhere. Weekend trips to Barcelona, Berlin, Rome, or the French countryside are normal.</p>

      <h2>9. Common Concerns (and Honest Answers)</h2>

      <h3>"French bureaucracy is a nightmare"</h3>
      <p>There is some truth to this, but it has improved dramatically for tech workers. The Passeport Talent was specifically designed to bypass much of the bureaucratic friction. Your employer will handle the heavy lifting, and most visa-sponsoring companies have dedicated relocation teams or use agencies like Settly or Relocate.me. The worst parts are usually dealing with the préfecture for renewals — annoying but manageable.</p>

      <h3>"Taxes are too high"</h3>
      <p>French income tax rates are progressive, with a top marginal rate of 45% (plus social charges). However, the effective tax rate for most software engineers (earning €50,000–€80,000) is 20–30%. And your taxes fund healthcare, retirement, and education that would cost you thousands per year in the US or UK. When you factor in total cost of living, most engineers are financially better off than they expect.</p>

      <h3>"Everyone speaks French"</h3>
      <p>At work, no — English is standard at international tech companies. Outside work, yes — but Paris is increasingly international, and apps like Google Translate and ChatGPT make daily life manageable even at zero French. That said, learning basic French is a good investment and Parisians genuinely appreciate the effort.</p>
