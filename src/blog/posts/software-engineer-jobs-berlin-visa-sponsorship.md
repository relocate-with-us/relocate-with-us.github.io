      <p>Berlin combines the scale of a global tech city with a cost of living that is still manageable compared to London, Munich, or Zurich. English is the working language at most product companies, and the German government actively routes skilled workers through the <strong>EU Blue Card</strong> and fast-track employment visa processes. If you are a software engineer targeting Berlin with visa sponsorship, this guide covers the hiring landscape, realistic salaries, housing, and how to avoid the most common application mistakes.</p>

      <h2>1. Why Berlin for international software engineers</h2>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Density of employers:</strong> Thousands of startups and scaleups, plus large engineering offices (N26, Zalando, Delivery Hero, Trade Republic, and many SaaS firms) mean more teams accustomed to sponsoring non-EU hires.</li>
        <li><strong>EU Blue Card:</strong> Germany is one of the most predictable EU Blue Card jurisdictions for IT — shortage-occupation salary thresholds are well documented and many engineering salaries qualify.</li>
        <li><strong>English-first engineering:</strong> You can ship production code for years before you need conversational German for daily life (though learning German still helps for bureaucracy and long-term integration).</li>
        <li><strong>Lower rent than Munich:</strong> Competition is fierce, but Berlin rents are still lower than Munich or Frankfurt for comparable neighbourhoods.</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>2. Visa pathways (high level)</h2>
      <p>For a standard full-time software job with a German employer, most engineers receive either the <strong>EU Blue Card</strong> or a <strong>skilled employment residence permit</strong>. Your employer typically initiates the process after you sign the contract. Processing times vary by embassy, but many candidates move within <strong>8–12 weeks</strong> when documents are complete.</p>
      <p>Key idea: your <strong>gross salary must meet the legal threshold</strong> for your visa category. IT roles often qualify under the <em>shortage occupation</em> rules, which use a lower threshold than the standard Blue Card track. Always confirm the exact figure with your employer's immigration partner — thresholds are updated periodically.</p>

      <h2>4. Types of companies most likely to sponsor</h2>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>VC-backed scaleups</strong> hiring across backend, platform, data, and security — they have immigration counsel on retainer.</li>
        <li><strong>Fintech and marketplaces</strong> with regulated components — they are used to compliance-heavy onboarding.</li>
        <li><strong>Global HQs with a Berlin engineering hub</strong> — mobility and relocation policies are already documented.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Smaller seed-stage startups <em>can</em> sponsor, but they may move more slowly if they have not hired non-EU staff before. Ask early whether they have a <strong>defined visa process</strong> and <strong>relocation budget</strong>.</p>

      <h2>5. Rent, neighbourhoods, and timing</h2>
      <p>Expect to spend significant time on housing. Many engineers secure a <strong>temporary corporate apartment</strong> or short-term furnished rental for the first 1–2 months while searching for a long-term lease. Documents landlords ask for often include your employment contract, SCHUFA (credit record), and proof of income — your HR team can usually provide a letter for the first month before your first payslip.</p>
      <p>Popular areas for tech workers include Prenzlauer Berg, Friedrichshain, Kreuzberg, Neukölln (north), and Charlottenburg — each balances commute, nightlife, and price differently.</p>

      <h2>6. Application strategy that actually works</h2>
      <ol class="space-y-3 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Lead with impact:</strong> German CVs are concise; use metrics (latency, reliability, revenue, MAU) rather than a generic skill list.</li>
        <li><strong>State work authorization honestly:</strong> "I require visa sponsorship" early in the process saves everyone time.</li>
        <li><strong>Prepare system design:</strong> Senior loops almost always include architecture and trade-off discussions.</li>
        <li><strong>Ask about relocation in the offer call:</strong> Lump sum, temporary housing, and immigration support should be explicit.</li>
      </ol>
