      <h2>1. Why Scandinavia? The Nordic Advantage</h2>
      <p>Before diving into specifics, here is why the Nordics stand out for software engineers compared to other European destinations:</p>

      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Work-life balance is policy, not a perk:</strong> Denmark pioneered the concept of "flexicurity" — flexible labour markets combined with strong social safety nets. In practice, this means most engineers work 37–40 hours per week, rarely work evenings or weekends, and take their full vacation entitlement without guilt</li>
        <li><strong>Parental leave measured in months:</strong> Sweden offers 480 days of paid parental leave per child (shared between parents). Denmark offers 52 weeks. Finland offers 320 days. These are not just policies on paper — engineers actually take them</li>
        <li><strong>Universal healthcare:</strong> All three countries provide universal, publicly funded healthcare. You will not worry about insurance networks, deductibles, or medical bankruptcy</li>
        <li><strong>Free or near-free education:</strong> If you have children, public schools in all three countries are world-class. International schools are available in Copenhagen, Stockholm, and Helsinki</li>
        <li><strong>Safety and trust:</strong> The Nordics consistently rank among the safest countries globally. Low crime, high social trust, clean environments, and excellent public infrastructure</li>
        <li><strong>English proficiency:</strong> Denmark, Sweden, and Finland rank in the top 10 globally for English proficiency. You can live and work entirely in English — unlike France or Germany, where language barriers can be an issue outside of work</li>
      </ul>

      <p>The trade-off? Higher taxes, long winters with limited daylight (November–February), and a culture that can feel reserved at first. But for engineers who prioritise balance, security, and quality of life over maximum cash compensation, the Nordics are hard to beat.</p>

      <!-- ==================== SECTION 2 ==================== -->
      <h2>2. Visa Options by Country</h2>

      <h3>Denmark: Fast-Track Scheme</h3>
      <p>Denmark's immigration system is designed for speed. The Fast-Track Scheme is specifically built for certified companies that need to hire international workers quickly.</p>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Processing time:</strong> Approximately 1 month — one of the fastest in Europe</li>
        <li><strong>Requirements:</strong> Job offer from a certified employer + salary above the threshold (currently DKK 375,000/year, approximately €50,000)</li>
        <li><strong>Duration:</strong> Up to 4 years, renewable</li>
        <li><strong>Family:</strong> Spouse and dependants can accompany you and work in Denmark</li>
        <li><strong>Path to permanent residency:</strong> After 4 years of continuous residence (with additional requirements including Danish language exam)</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Denmark also offers a <strong>Pay Limit Scheme</strong> for roles paying above DKK 500,000/year (~€67,000) — most senior engineering roles qualify. And an <strong>EU Blue Card</strong> pathway for those planning to move within the EU later.</p>

      <h3>Sweden: Work Permit</h3>
      <p>Sweden's work permit system is straightforward but slower than Denmark's.</p>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Requirements:</strong> A job offer from a Swedish employer, salary and working conditions matching Swedish collective agreements (typically SEK 28,000+/month), and health insurance for the first period</li>
        <li><strong>Processing time:</strong> 2–4 months (the Swedish Migration Agency has been working to reduce this)</li>
        <li><strong>Duration:</strong> 2 years initially, renewable for up to 4 years total</li>
        <li><strong>Family:</strong> Spouse and children under 21 can accompany you</li>
        <li><strong>Permanent residency:</strong> After 4 years of continuous employment in Sweden</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Finland: Specialist Residence Permit</h3>
      <p>Finland has streamlined its work-based immigration significantly. The Specialist Residence Permit is designed for skilled workers including software engineers.</p>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Requirements:</strong> Job offer from a Finnish employer, specialist qualifications (a degree or relevant experience), minimum salary of €3,473/month (2026 threshold)</li>
        <li><strong>Processing time:</strong> 2–4 weeks for the D visa (express processing). Finland aims for the fastest processing in the Nordics</li>
        <li><strong>Duration:</strong> Up to 2 years initially, renewable</li>
        <li><strong>Family:</strong> Spouse and dependants can apply simultaneously</li>
        <li><strong>Permanent residency:</strong> After 4 years of continuous residence</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>6. Life as an Engineer in the Nordics</h2>

      <h3>Work Culture</h3>
      <p>Nordic work culture is genuinely different from the rest of Europe, and miles apart from Silicon Valley. The key characteristics:</p>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Flat hierarchies:</strong> Junior engineers speak up in meetings. Managers are facilitators, not directors. Your title matters less than your ideas</li>
        <li><strong>Trust-based:</strong> Nobody watches your screen or tracks your hours. Output matters, not presence. Remote and hybrid work are standard</li>
        <li><strong>No overwork culture:</strong> Leaving at 5 PM is normal. Taking your full vacation is expected. Burnout is taken seriously as a systemic problem, not a personal failing</li>
        <li><strong>Fika (Sweden) / Hygge (Denmark):</strong> Regular coffee breaks with colleagues are a cultural institution. These informal moments build stronger teams than forced team-building events ever could</li>
        <li><strong>Consensus-driven decisions:</strong> Decisions take longer because everyone's input is sought. This can frustrate engineers from more hierarchical cultures, but it produces better outcomes and stronger buy-in</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>The Winter Question</h3>
      <p>Every article about the Nordics has to address this honestly: winters are dark. In Stockholm, December gives you about 6 hours of daylight. Helsinki is similar. Copenhagen gets slightly more light but remains grey from November to February.</p>
      <p>How engineers cope:</p>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li>Light therapy lamps (many offices have them)</li>
        <li>Outdoor sports — cross-country skiing, ice swimming (Finland's national pastime), winter cycling</li>
        <li>The "hygge" approach — embrace indoor coziness with candles, warm drinks, and socialising</li>
        <li>Using your generous vacation days for winter escapes to Southern Europe</li>
      </ul>

      <p>The flip side: Nordic summers are magical. 18–20 hours of daylight, warm weather, and the entire culture moves outdoors. Swedish Midsommar, Finnish lake cottages, Copenhagen harbour swimming — the long summer days make winter worth enduring.</p>

      <h3>Social Life</h3>
      <p>The stereotype of cold, distant Scandinavians has some basis in reality — making friends with locals can take time. But the international tech communities in Copenhagen, Stockholm, and Helsinki are large and welcoming. Meetups, sports clubs, language exchange groups, and coworking events are easy ways to build a social circle. Many companies also have active social committees that organise events.</p>
