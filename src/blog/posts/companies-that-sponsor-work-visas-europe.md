      <h2>1. Germany — The Largest Market for Visa Sponsorship</h2>
      <p>Germany is the top destination for international tech workers in Europe, and for good reason. It has the continent's largest economy, a massive and growing tech sector, and one of the most accessible visa systems in the world — the EU Blue Card. Berlin alone has over 500 tech startups hiring in English, and cities like Munich, Hamburg, and Walldorf are home to major global tech companies.</p>
      <p>Germany's <a href="/blog/posts/how-to-get-a-job-in-germany/" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">EU Blue Card</a> requires a recognised university degree and a job offer with a minimum salary of approximately €45,300 (or ~€41,000 for shortage occupations like IT). The IT Specialist Visa lets experienced professionals without a degree qualify if they have 3+ years of experience and a salary above ~€51,000. Both paths lead to permanent residency within 21–33 months.</p>

      <h2>2. Netherlands — High Salaries and the 30% Ruling</h2>
      <p>The Netherlands, and Amsterdam in particular, is one of Europe's most attractive destinations for international tech workers. The country has a highly international workforce — English is widely spoken — and Amsterdam is home to major tech companies, fintech unicorns, and trading firms that pay among the highest salaries in Europe.</p>
      <p>The Netherlands offers a significant tax advantage for international hires: the <strong>30% ruling</strong>. Under this scheme, eligible expats receive 30% of their gross salary tax-free for up to five years. On a €90,000 salary, this means roughly €27,000 of your income is untaxed — a significant boost to your take-home pay. To qualify, you must be hired from abroad (or within 150 km of the Dutch border doesn't count) and have specific expertise that is scarce in the Dutch labour market.</p>

      <div class="bg-yellow-50 dark:bg-yellow-900/20 border-l-4 border-yellow-400 dark:border-yellow-600 p-4 rounded-r-lg mb-6">
        <p class="text-sm text-gray-700 dark:text-gray-300 mb-0"><strong>30% ruling note:</strong> The Dutch government has been gradually reducing the 30% ruling. As of 2024, the full 30% applies for the first 20 months, then drops to 20% for the next 20 months, and 10% for the final 20 months. Confirm the latest rules with your employer before relying on this benefit.</p>

      <p>The Dutch work visa process is employer-driven. Your company applies for a <strong>Highly Skilled Migrant (Kennismigrant)</strong> permit on your behalf. Processing is fast — typically 2–4 weeks — and the permit allows your partner to work freely. The main requirement is a salary above ~€5,000/month (for workers over 30) or ~€3,700/month (under 30). Most tech salaries easily exceed this threshold.</p>

      <!-- ==================== UNITED KINGDOM ==================== -->
      <h2>3. United Kingdom — The Skilled Worker Visa</h2>
      <p>The UK left the EU, but it remains one of the most active tech markets in Europe. London is the largest tech hub on the continent, home to hundreds of fintech companies, AI startups, and global engineering offices. Post-Brexit, the UK introduced the <strong>Skilled Worker visa</strong>, which replaced the old Tier 2 visa. The process is well-established and thousands of tech workers receive sponsorship every year.</p>
      <p>To hire an international worker, the company must hold a <strong>Sponsor Licence</strong> from UK Visas and Immigration. You can check whether a company is a licensed sponsor on the <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/register-of-licensed-sponsors-workers" target="_blank" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">UK government's public register</a>. All companies listed below hold this licence.</p>

      <div class="bg-yellow-50 dark:bg-yellow-900/20 border-l-4 border-yellow-400 dark:border-yellow-600 p-4 rounded-r-lg mb-6">
        <p class="text-sm text-gray-700 dark:text-gray-300 mb-0"><strong>UK salary thresholds:</strong> The Skilled Worker visa requires a minimum salary of £38,700 (as of 2024) or the "going rate" for the role, whichever is higher. Most tech roles in London exceed this comfortably. The visa application fee is £719–£1,420, plus the Immigration Health Surcharge (£1,035/year). Many employers cover these costs.</p>

The city combines a world-class quality of life — Mediterranean climate, affordable cost of living (compared to London or Amsterdam), and rich culture — with an increasingly vibrant startup ecosystem. Salaries are lower than Germany or the Netherlands (senior engineers typically earn €50,000–€75,000), but the cost of living is also significantly lower.</p>

      <h2>6. How to Tell If a Company Sponsors Visas</h2>
      <p>Not every company openly advertises visa sponsorship on their careers page. Here is how to figure out whether a company will sponsor your visa before you invest time applying.</p>

      <h3>Look for explicit signals</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>Job listing language:</strong> Look for phrases like "visa sponsorship available," "relocation support," "we welcome international applicants," or "we handle the work permit process." Some companies tag jobs with a visa icon or "relocation" badge</li>
        <li><strong>Company careers page:</strong> Check the FAQ or "Working at [Company]" section. Many companies have a dedicated relocation or visa information page</li>
        <li><strong>Glassdoor and Blind reviews:</strong> Search for "visa" or "sponsorship" in employee reviews. International employees often mention the visa process in their reviews</li>
        <li><strong>LinkedIn:</strong> Look at the company's employees. If they have engineers from your country working there, it is a strong signal that they sponsor visas</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Check public registers</h3>
      <ul class="space-y-2 mb-6">
        <li><strong>UK:</strong> The <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/register-of-licensed-sponsors-workers" target="_blank" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">Register of Licensed Sponsors</a> is public. You can download it and search for any UK company to see if they hold a sponsor licence</li>
        <li><strong>Germany:</strong> There is no public register, but any company that meets the EU Blue Card salary threshold can sponsor you. The employer doesn't need a special licence</li>
        <li><strong>Netherlands:</strong> The <a href="https://ind.nl/en/public-register-recognised-sponsors" target="_blank" class="text-brand-600 dark:text-brand-400 hover:underline font-medium">IND Public Register of Recognised Sponsors</a> lists all companies approved to hire Highly Skilled Migrants</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Ask directly — but strategically</h3>
      <p>If the job listing does not mention visa sponsorship, apply anyway if you are a strong fit. Many companies decide on a case-by-case basis. The best time to raise the visa question is during the <strong>recruiter screen</strong> — state it clearly and confidently: "I would need visa sponsorship to work in [country]. Is this something your company supports?"</p>
      <p>Do not lead with the visa question in your cover letter or application. Lead with your skills and experience. Bring up the visa after they are already interested in you.</p>

      <h2>7. How to Apply Successfully</h2>
      <p>Knowing which companies sponsor visas is only half the battle. You also need to stand out in a competitive international applicant pool. Here are the strategies that work.</p>

        <li><strong>General:</strong> Always mention your visa eligibility clearly — "Eligible for the EU Blue Card" or "Currently hold a valid work permit for [country]" — in your CV summary or cover letter</li>
