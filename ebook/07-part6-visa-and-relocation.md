# Part 6 — Visa and relocation

Once you have an offer, the visa process begins. Most candidates panic at this stage because the documentation looks overwhelming. It's not. Each visa has a known checklist, a known timeline, and a known cost. Here are the cheat sheets.

> **Disclaimer:** Visa rules change. The numbers in this chapter are accurate as of late 2025/early 2026. Always confirm the current numbers on the official government source linked at the end of each section before you make decisions.

## Chapter 16. Visa pathways compared

### Germany — EU Blue Card

**Who qualifies:**
- Recognised Bachelor's degree (German or equivalent foreign degree, must appear in [anabin database](https://anabin.kmk.org))
- Job offer with minimum gross salary of €45,934.20/year (2026) for IT shortage occupations, €50,700/year general
- Employment contract for at least 6 months

**Process:**
1. Get offer
2. Employer signs employment contract
3. Apply at German consulate in your home country (or at the local Ausländerbehörde if already in Germany on a different status)
4. Submit: passport, contract, degree, anabin printout, photos, application fee €100
5. Decision in 1–8 weeks (most consulates: 4 weeks)
6. Enter Germany, register address (*Anmeldung*) within 14 days
7. Pick up biometric residence permit at Ausländerbehörde

**Family:** Spouse can join, can work without restriction. Kids covered.

**Path to PR:** 21 months on Blue Card with B1 German, 27 months without. After PR, citizenship in further 5 years (3 with strong integration). Germany now allows dual citizenship since 2024.

**Official source:** [Make-it-in-Germany — EU Blue Card](https://www.make-it-in-germany.com/en/visa-residence/types/eu-blue-card)

### Germany — IT Specialist Visa (no degree required)

**Who qualifies:**
- 3+ years of relevant IT work experience in the past 7 years
- Job classified under ISCO-08 group 25 (IT specialists)
- Annual gross salary ≥ €45,934.20 (2026)
- Employment contract for at least 6 months

**Process:** Same as Blue Card but without degree recognition step. Slightly more discretion at the consulate.

**Why it matters:** Self-taught engineers, bootcamp grads, and those with non-recognised degrees can use this. Underused, very real path.

### Netherlands — Highly Skilled Migrant (Kennismigrant)

**Who qualifies:**
- Job offer at a Recognised Sponsor (check the [IND public register](https://ind.nl/en/public-register-recognised-sponsors))
- Salary thresholds (2026 monthly gross, excluding 8% holiday allowance):
  - **30+:** €5,942/month
  - **Under 30:** €4,357/month
  - **Orientation Year graduates:** €3,122/month

**Process:**
1. Employer files with IND — they upload your documents
2. IND decides in 2 weeks (often faster)
3. Visa pickup at consulate or Schengen entry
4. Register at municipality (BSN), then biometrics in NL
5. Receive residence permit card in ~2 weeks

**Family:** Spouse / partner included, can work without restriction.

**The 30% ruling:** Apply within 4 months of arrival. Up to 30% of gross salary tax-free for ~5 years (transitioning to 27% from 2027). Capped at €78,600 tax-free per year (2026). For most tech salaries, this is €15–25k of tax-free income annually.

**Path to PR:** 5 years. Dutch citizenship in further 5 years (allowed dual citizenship in some cases).

**Official source:** [IND — Highly Skilled Migrant](https://ind.nl/en/highly-skilled-migrant)

### United Kingdom — Skilled Worker visa

**Who qualifies:**
- Job offer at a company with an active A-rated Sponsor Licence (check the [official register](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/register-of-licensed-sponsors-workers))
- Job in eligible occupation list (most tech roles qualify)
- Salary ≥ £38,700 OR the "going rate" for the SOC code, whichever is higher
- B1 English (most native English speakers and CS graduates from English-medium programs qualify automatically)

**Process:**
1. Employer issues Certificate of Sponsorship (CoS) with a unique reference number
2. You apply online; submit biometrics
3. Pay: visa fee £719–£1,420 + Immigration Health Surcharge £1,035/year × visa duration (so £3,105 for 3 years, £5,175 for 5 years)
4. Decision in 3–8 weeks (priority service available, ~5 working days for extra ~£500)
5. Receive Biometric Residence Permit on entry

**Family:** Dependent partner + kids visa available. Partner can work freely.

**Path to PR:** 5 years (Indefinite Leave to Remain). British citizenship in further 1 year.

**Important nuances:**
- IHS is a *huge* upfront cost — most reputable employers cover it for the worker; few cover for dependents. Negotiate.
- Switching jobs requires a new CoS from the new employer; you can't move freely.
- Your time on a Skilled Worker visa counts toward ILR even if you switch employers.

**Official source:** [Gov.uk — Skilled Worker visa](https://www.gov.uk/skilled-worker-visa)

### United States — H-1B (and alternatives)

**The H-1B reality:**
- Annual cap: 65,000 + 20,000 for US Master's holders
- ~3× oversubscribed every year — selection by lottery
- Lottery happens in March, results in late March/April, work start October 1
- Some employers (universities, non-profits, research institutions) are **cap-exempt** — apply year-round, no lottery

**Who qualifies:**
- Bachelor's degree (or equivalent — generally 3 years of professional experience = 1 year of education for the equivalent calculation)
- Job offer in a "specialty occupation"
- Salary at or above the prevailing wage for the role and location (set by DOL)
- Employer files Labor Condition Application + H-1B petition

**Cost:** Employer pays $4,000–$8,000 in fees + legal. You pay $0 (legally — it's the employer's burden).

**Family:** H-4 visa for spouse and kids under 21. H-4 EAD allows spouse to work *only if* the H-1B holder has an approved I-140 (so practically: not for the first 1–3 years).

**Path to green card:** Employer files I-140 (PERM); after approval, you wait for a "current" priority date based on country of birth:
- Most countries: 1–2 years
- India: 50+ years for EB-2/EB-3 (yes, really)
- China: 5–10 years

**Better US alternatives if eligible:**
- **O-1** — extraordinary ability. Real for: published researchers, well-known open-source maintainers, awards, prominent talks/articles, top-tier school faculty.
- **L-1** — internal transfer. If your current employer has a US office, transfer.
- **TN** — Canadian/Mexican citizens, NAFTA-based. Easy.
- **E-3** — Australians. Easier than H-1B.
- **H-1B1** — Singapore/Chile. Bilateral, easy.
- **EB-1A / EB-1B / NIW** — green card paths for high-achievers. Self-petition possible (EB-1A and NIW). 1–2 year process.

**Official source:** [USCIS — H-1B](https://www.uscis.gov/h-1b-cap-season)

### Switzerland — B-permit (and L-permit)

**Who qualifies (non-EU/EFTA):**
- Job offer; employer must justify why a Swiss/EU candidate isn't available
- Quotas exist (~8,500 B-permits/year for non-EU/EFTA, split across cantons)
- Salary "in line with industry and local norms" — practically CHF 90k+ for tech, often CHF 130k+ for senior

**Process:**
1. Employer applies to cantonal labour office for a work permit
2. Decision typically 4–12 weeks
3. Once approved, you apply for visa at consulate (1–4 weeks)
4. Enter Switzerland, register at Gemeinde (municipality) within 14 days
5. Receive B-permit (renewable annually, then converts to C-permit / PR after 10 years, or 5 if from privileged country)

**Family:** Spouse can join, can work freely. Kids covered.

**Path to PR (C-permit):**
- Most countries: 10 years
- Privileged countries (US, Canada, UK, EU/EFTA): 5 years
- Citizenship: further 10 years usually, demanding cultural and language tests

**Why it's worth the friction:** Swiss tech compensation is unmatched in Europe (Google Zurich staff: $400k+ TC), and the quality of life ranks consistently top-3 globally.

**Official source:** [State Secretariat for Migration (SEM)](https://www.sem.admin.ch)

### Ireland — Critical Skills Employment Permit

**Who qualifies:**
- Job offer for an occupation on the Critical Skills List (most tech roles included)
- Salary ≥ €38,000/year for ICT roles, €64,000+ for the unrestricted CSEP
- Relevant qualifications

**Process:**
1. Employer applies to Department of Enterprise (DETE)
2. Decision in 4–8 weeks
3. You apply for the visa
4. Enter Ireland, register with GNIB

**Family:** Spouse / partner can work freely after a separate immediate Stamp 1 process — much faster than UK/US.

**Path to PR:** 5 years. Irish citizenship after 5 years residency (you also become an EU citizen — useful).

**Official source:** [DETE — Employment Permits](https://enterprise.gov.ie/en/what-we-do/workplace-and-skills/employment-permits)

### Sweden — Work permit

**Who qualifies:**
- Employment offer with salary ≥ SEK 28,480/month (2026) for general work permit
- Insurance covering you for the duration

**Process:**
1. Employer offers job and applies online to Swedish Migration Agency
2. Decision in 1–4 months for "Certified Operators," 4–8 months otherwise
3. Visa issued; you enter Sweden; register at Skatteverket for personnummer

**Family:** Spouse / partner can join and work.

**Path to PR:** 4 years (faster than most of EU).

**Official source:** [Migrationsverket](https://www.migrationsverket.se/English)

### Canada — Express Entry / Global Talent Stream

**Express Entry:** Points-based (CRS). Apply through pool, get an Invitation to Apply when your CRS is high enough for the draw. Most tech workers in 2024-26 needed 470+ CRS for general draws; category-specific draws (e.g., STEM) sometimes go lower (~440–470).

**Global Talent Stream (GTS):** Employer-driven, fast (10 working days for many cases). Used by Shopify, Wealthsimple, etc. Best path if you have an offer in hand.

**Path to PR:** GTS holders → Express Entry → PR within 1–2 years total. Then citizenship in further 3 years.

**Official source:** [IRCC — Express Entry](https://www.canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship/services/immigrate-canada/express-entry.html)

### Australia — Subclass 482 (employer-sponsored) / 189 / 186

**482 — Temporary Skill Shortage:** Employer-sponsored, 2 or 4 years, renewable. Most common for tech.

**189 — Skilled Independent:** Points-based, no employer needed. Useful if your occupation is on the SOL (Skilled Occupation List) and you score well.

**186 — Employer Nomination Scheme:** Path to PR via employer sponsorship after a couple of years.

**Official source:** [Department of Home Affairs](https://immi.homeaffairs.gov.au)

## Chapter 17. Document checklist and timeline

### The "have ready before you apply" pile

Get these together *before* you start interviewing seriously. They take time and bureaucracy.

- **Passport** — valid for at least 18 months from intended start date, ideally 24+
- **Birth certificate** — original; some countries want apostilled
- **Marriage certificate** (if applicable) — apostilled for visa applications in DE/CH/IE/UK
- **Bachelor's / Master's diploma + transcript** — official certified copies
- **Degree recognition / equivalency** (if applicable):
  - Germany: anabin database printout, or ZAB Statement of Comparability
  - Switzerland: SEM equivalence (sometimes)
  - Australia: VETASSESS
  - Canada: WES or ICAS evaluation
- **Criminal record check / Police clearance** — from your home country (and any country you've lived in for 12+ months in the past 5–10 years). Often called *Polizeiliches Führungszeugnis* (DE), DBS check (UK), Police Clearance Certificate (varies).
- **Reference letters** from previous employers (helpful in EU)
- **Tax documents from previous employers** — last 2 years of tax returns / payslips (some visa applications require)
- **Health insurance** — proof of coverage during transition period
- **Bank statements** — last 3–6 months (some applications require proof of funds)
- **Apostille or legalisation** of any foreign-issued document destined for an official use — Hague Convention apostille for most EU countries.

Get apostilles done at home before leaving. Doing them retroactively from abroad is a nightmare.

### Timeline: from offer to first day at the new job

Realistic timelines, by country:

**Netherlands:** 4–8 weeks
- Week 0: offer accepted, employer files Kennismigrant
- Week 2–4: IND approval
- Week 4–6: visa stamp at consulate
- Week 6–8: arrive, register, biometrics, start work

**Germany:** 6–12 weeks
- Week 0–2: offer accepted, contract signed, anabin done
- Week 2–4: consulate appointment booked (long wait sometimes)
- Week 4–10: consulate decision
- Week 10–12: arrive, *Anmeldung*, residence permit card

**United Kingdom:** 4–12 weeks
- Week 0–2: offer accepted, employer issues CoS
- Week 2–4: visa application + biometrics
- Week 4–8: standard decision (or 5 days with priority service)
- Week 8–12: arrive, BRP collection

**Switzerland:** 8–16 weeks
- Cantonal review takes the longest. Plan for 3–4 months.

**United States H-1B:** ~6–9 months from offer
- October 1 start at earliest if filed in March lottery
- Premium processing (15-day decision) is available for an extra $2,500 fee

**Ireland:** 6–10 weeks
- CSEP decision in ~4–6 weeks, then visa stamp.

### What to do *during* the wait

1. **Take a vacation.** Seriously. The first month at a new role abroad will be exhausting. Rest.
2. **Settle your current life.** Notice on rental, bank account closure plans, car sale, pet relocation.
3. **Open a [Wise](https://wise.com) / [Revolut](https://revolut.com) account.** Multi-currency, low fees, useful for receiving first salary while opening local bank.
4. **Research housing.** Most relocators use Airbnb/temporary accommodation for first 30–60 days, then apartment-hunt in person.
5. **Start the language.** Even 30 minutes/day of Duolingo / Pimsleur is worth it.
6. **Tax planning.** Your home country may want exit tax / final return. Get advice if you have significant assets.
7. **Goodbyes.** Throw a party. You'll miss people more than you think.

## Chapter 18. Logistics cheat sheet

The boring stuff that destroys most relocations. Keep this short and actionable.

### Housing — the one rule

**Stay in temporary accommodation 30–60 days, then apartment-hunt in person.** Every other strategy (long-distance search, Airbnb-to-permanent, agency) produces worse results. Use Spotahome, HomeLike, or Wunderflats (DE) for medium-term furnished lets. Ask your company if they have a relocation partner — many do.

City-specific warnings:
- **Amsterdam / Berlin / Munich:** brutal rental markets. Get your Schufa (DE) / BSN (NL) sorted first — landlords reject applicants who don't have them. Use Pararius (NL), Funda, ImmoScout24 (DE).
- **Zurich:** you often need a Swiss bank account to sign a lease. Your employer can help bridge this.
- **London:** typical hunt is 4–6 weeks. Deposit capped at 5 weeks' rent (tenant fees mostly illegal since 2019).
- **US cities:** be ready for 1–2 months' deposit upfront. Zillow, Apartments.com, Streeteasy (NYC).

### Banking — do this in week 1

1. **Before you arrive:** open a Wise or Revolut account. Use it for salary bridging while your local account processes.
2. **Day 1 in the new country:** open a local account. Payroll, rent, and utilities won't go to a foreign account.
   - DE: N26 or DKB (online) / Sparkasse (slowest, but most landlords accept it)
   - NL: ING or ABN AMRO
   - UK: Monzo or Starling (open remotely before you arrive)
   - US: Chase or Bank of America (in person, needs SSN)
   - CH: UBS or Raiffeisen

### Healthcare — enrol in week 1, not week 4

- **DE:** mandatory. Enrol with TK or Barmer within 14 days of starting work.
- **NL:** mandatory. Choose a *basisverzekering* insurer (~€140/month). Fine if you miss it.
- **UK:** NHS is pre-paid via the IHS on your visa. Just register with a GP in your area.
- **US:** enrol during your employer's onboarding window — you can't enrol later without a qualifying life event.
- **CH:** mandatory private insurance (~CHF 400–600/month). Compare on [Comparis.ch](https://comparis.ch).

### Taxes — the only three things to know

1. **You're tax-resident wherever you spend >183 days/year.** File accordingly.
2. **Most countries have double-taxation treaties.** You won't pay twice, but you may file in two countries in year one. Get a local tax adviser — costs €300–€800, saves multiples.
3. **US citizens:** you file US taxes for life no matter where you live. If you have foreign accounts >$10k, file FBAR (FinCEN 114) every year. Penalties for missing it are severe. Hire a CPA who specialises in expats.
