# Part 5 — Interviews and offers

## Chapter 13. The international interview prep system

International tech interviews assess three layers, in this order:

1. **Will you actually move and stay?** (The visa/commitment screen.)
2. **Can you communicate well across cultures?** (The collaboration screen.)
3. **Can you do the job?** (The technical screen.)

Domestic candidates only have to clear layer 3. You have to clear all three. Most candidates over-prepare for layer 3 and under-prepare for 1 and 2.

### The standard pipeline

Most companies follow some version of:

1. **Recruiter screen** — 25–45 minutes, phone or video. Visa, motivation, salary, basic fit. Sometimes a few softball technical questions.
2. **Hiring manager screen** — 30–60 minutes, video. Fit, leadership, recent work, deeper motivation.
3. **Technical screens** — 1–4 rounds depending on company:
   - Coding (live or take-home)
   - System design (mid+ levels)
   - Domain-specific (ML, infra, security)
4. **Behavioural / values round** — 45–60 minutes
5. **Final / offer call** — comp discussion

Total elapsed time: 2–6 weeks. Your job is to keep momentum at each stage.

### The 90-minute prep block (per company)

Block this into your calendar before each loop. Don't wing it.

**0–20 minutes — Company research**
- Last 2 quarterly investor calls (if public) or last big news (if private)
- Latest tech blog posts
- Recent open-source releases / PRs from their engineers
- Glassdoor / Blind reviews of the interview process — interviewer questions are often discussed there
- Their values / culture page

**20–35 minutes — Role and team research**
- Re-read the JD line by line
- Find the team's recent talks, papers, podcasts
- Identify the manager and 2–3 ICs from LinkedIn; skim what they post

**35–55 minutes — Your stories**
- Pick 6 STAR stories from your master resume that map to common questions:
  - Hardest technical problem you've solved
  - Biggest impact you've had
  - Conflict resolved with a colleague
  - Failure and what you learned
  - Cross-cultural / cross-timezone collaboration
  - Feedback received and applied
- Write each in STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result), 90 seconds spoken

**55–70 minutes — Questions you'll ask them**
- 5–8 questions, ranked from "must ask" to "nice to ask"
- Mix of: technical (how do you handle X?), team (what's your on-call rotation like?), strategy (where's the company going?), and personal (what do *you* love about working here?)

**70–90 minutes — Logistics + dress rehearsal**
- Test your microphone, lighting, internet (use [speedtest.net](https://speedtest.net))
- Practice introducing yourself in 90 seconds
- Practice the visa/relocation answer aloud
- Dress at least one level above what you'd wear to your day job

### Common questions, with international twists

**"Tell me about yourself."**
60–90 seconds, structured: (1) where I am now and what I do, (2) how I got here in 2–3 sentences, (3) why I'm talking to you specifically. End with the relocation/visa pivot: "...which is why I'm specifically targeting [country] — the [team / problem space] is where I want to be next."

**"Why our company?"**
This is where you prove you read more than the careers page. Reference a specific recent thing they shipped, a paper, a value. Then connect it to your skills.

**"Why are you leaving your current role?"**
Be honest but neutral. Never trash-talk current/past employers. Frame it as positive pull, not negative push: "I've grown a lot at [current company], and the next thing I want to do is [X]. I'm not seeing a path to it here, so I'm looking outside."

**"Why this country specifically?"**
This is the visa-commitment proxy question. Don't say "anywhere is fine" — that signals flight risk. Pick 1–2 specific reasons (the tech ecosystem, a city you've visited, a domain that's strong there) and 1 lifestyle reason (climate, family ties, you've been learning the language). Be specific.

**"How do you handle working across time zones / with people from different cultures?"**
If you've done it: tell a story. If you haven't: talk about how you'd approach it (async-first, clear written communication, over-communicating context, respecting holidays).

**"Tell me about a conflict with a colleague."**
Pick one where you came across as the reasonable adult. Describe the situation, your role, what you did to de-escalate, and the outcome. *Never* describe a conflict where you "won" — that signals you're hard to work with.

### Live coding interviews

The general advice (Cracking the Coding Interview, NeoCode, etc.) applies. The international-specific addendum:

- **Speak in English even if you're better in your native language.** Practice this — the cognitive load of solving a problem *and* speaking English is higher than either alone.
- **Don't apologise for accent or grammar mistakes.** Just keep going.
- **Verbalise your thinking.** Most interviewers grade *how* you solve as much as *what* you produce.
- **Ask clarifying questions before coding.** Especially valuable for non-native speakers — it gives you time to gather thoughts and shows seniority.

### System design interviews

Standard advice (Alex Xu's books, ByteByteGo, the Hello Interview YouTube channel) is excellent. The international add: be ready to discuss systems at the scale common in your *target* country. If you're going to Booking.com, know travel-industry-scale traffic patterns. If you're going to Adyen, know payments at high TPS. If you're going to ASML, know embedded constraints.

### Behavioural / values rounds

These are often the deciding round for visa-sponsored hires, because companies want to be sure they're investing in someone who'll *stay*. Be prepared for:

- "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" — pin yourself to *this country / this company* concretely
- "How would your team describe you?" — be specific, give examples
- "What's something you disagreed with at work, and how did you handle it?"
- "Tell me about a time you mentored someone."
- "Tell me about a time you missed a deadline."

The pattern: show humility, ownership, growth, and stability.

## Chapter 14. Country-by-country interview norms

Every market has unwritten rules. Get them wrong and you fail interviews you should pass.

### United States
Direct, fast-paced, metrics-heavy. Lead every answer with the result, then back into the story. Show ambition — "I want to be a Staff Engineer in 3 years" is a good answer, not arrogant. Read the company's values page (Amazon Leadership Principles, Meta's cultural values) and prepare 1 STAR story per principle. Energy and eye contact are expected, not aggressive.

### United Kingdom
Slightly more formal, slightly more reserved. Don't oversell — Brits sniff out hype quickly. Small talk at the start is expected; jumping straight to achievements seems brusque. Self-deprecation works lightly; doing it repeatedly looks insecure. Dry humour is fine if it's actually funny. Don't force it.

### Germany
Be precise. Be on time. Bring documents. Expect deep technical questions — "because it felt right" fails; "because the latency budget was X and option Y satisfied that constraint" passes. Some interviewers skip small talk entirely; this is efficiency, not rudeness. Germans respect candour about gaps and weaknesses far more than polished answers. Address the visa timeline proactively if the interviewer seems uncertain about it.

### Netherlands
Blunt feedback in an interview means they like you — "that's not quite right" is respect. Engineering managers are peers, not bosses; push back on things you disagree with. Practical trumps flashy: "how will this work in production" matters more than clever design. English is the working language at essentially all tech companies.

### Switzerland
Conservative dress, formal address ("Herr/Frau [Surname]") until invited otherwise. Don't open with comp questions — Swiss are private about money; let them broach it. Concise, considered answers beat long ones. Even A2-level German or French is noticed positively.

### Quick notes for other markets

| Country | One thing to know |
|---------|------------------|
| France | "Vous" not "tu" until told. Education prestige matters. Intellectual rigour over speed. Confirm if interview is in French or English. |
| Sweden / Denmark | Flat hierarchy, first names with everyone. Modesty works. Don't pitch yourself as always-on — they value work-life balance. Punctuality is non-negotiable. |
| Ireland | Warm and chatty, but multinational hiring bar is FAANG-level. Don't let the friendliness fool you. CSEP eligibility comes up early. |
| Spain / Portugal | Warmer, relational opening is expected. Decision cycles are slower — "next week" often means next month. Benefits (vacation days, healthcare) matter as much as salary. |
| Australia | Casual and direct. References will actually be called — have 2–3 ready. Know your visa subclass number (482, 189, 186) — saying it correctly signals you're not a flight risk. |

## Chapter 15. Salary research and negotiation across borders

You will be paid less than you're worth if you don't research before the offer call. International candidates especially get lowballed because they don't know the market.

### The salary research stack

**Levels.fyi** — best for FAANG and large public tech. Strong on US, growing on EU. Free.

![Levels.fyi — European city salary comparison](../screenshots/ss-15-levelsfyi-europe.png)
*Filter by "Europe" and sort by Total Comp. Note the massive gap between Zurich (Google/Meta) and Berlin mid-market — Zurich can be 2–3× for the same level.*

**Glassdoor** — wide coverage, lower data quality. Cross-reference with others.

**Blind** — anonymous tech employee discussion. Search "[Company] offer" or "[Company] negotiation" — real numbers buried in threads. Free.

### How to read salary data

Three rules:

1. **Compare TC, not base.** Total comp = base + bonus + equity + benefits. In the US, equity often matches base at senior levels. In EU, base + bonus dominates; equity is smaller and often illiquid.

2. **Compare net, not gross.** A €120k Munich salary and a €100k Amsterdam salary (with 30% ruling) net out almost identical. Always run the numbers through a calculator.

3. **Compare by city, not country.** Berlin and Munich are 25% apart in COL. Lisbon and Porto, similar. London is its own universe.

### Salary expectations by stage

**During application or recruiter screen** — the recruiter will ask "what are your salary expectations?" Two strategies:

*The range strategy:* "Based on my research for this role and city, I'd expect [low end of range] to [high end of range]. The exact number depends on the full package and equity component. What's the band for this role?"

*The deflection strategy:* "I'd love to learn more about the role first. Can you share the band you're working with?" — works best in EU, where bands are often shared. Less in US.

Either way, *never* give a single number. Always a range, and always with the rationale ("based on [data source]").

**During offer negotiation** — once you have an offer in hand:

1. **Don't accept on the call.** "Thanks so much, this is exciting. I want to take 24–48 hours to review the full package — can I come back to you by [day]?"
2. **Decide your three numbers** before the negotiation:
   - **Walk-away:** below this, you say no.
   - **Acceptable:** the number where you'd take it.
   - **Reach:** the number you'd celebrate.
3. **Counter once.** Email > phone for the counter (so you can be precise). Phrase it as: "Thanks for the offer. I'm enthusiastic about the role. To bridge the gap to where I'd accept, I'd ask for [specific number] base, [specific equity], and [specific relocation]."
4. **Justify with data.** "Based on [Levels.fyi / Honeypot / equivalent] data for [role] at [seniority] in [city], market is [range]. My [years] of experience and [specific achievements] place me in the upper half of that range."
5. **Give them a path to yes.** "If base is fixed, I'd be open to [more equity / signing bonus / relocation / additional vacation]."
6. **Be ready for "this is our best offer."** It may genuinely be. Ask for time, then decide based on your three numbers.

### What to negotiate beyond base

In rough order of how often companies will move on these:

1. **Signing bonus** — easiest yes; doesn't affect future budget cycles.
2. **Equity grant** — second easiest at private companies; harder at public.
3. **Relocation package** — often expandable, especially for international hires (visa fees, flights, temp accommodation).
4. **Start date / vacation days** — easy adjustments.
5. **Title / level** — moderately hard but the highest-leverage long-term ask.
6. **Base salary** — hardest because it sets future budget and band fairness.
7. **Remote / hybrid days** — variable; ask if it matters.
8. **Education budget / conferences** — many companies have a yearly budget; some are flexible.

### The relocation-specific negotiation

Always negotiate visa and relocation with the same data-driven specificity as salary:

- Ask for **legal fees fully covered** (visa + dependents)
- Ask for **flights for you + partner + kids** (one-way + one round-trip "look-see" visit)
- Ask for **30–60 days temporary accommodation** in target city
- Ask for **one shipping container** if you're moving with furniture
- Ask for **first-year tax filing assistance** (companies like KPMG / EY / PwC have flat-fee expat packages)
- Ask for **30% ruling application support** (NL)
- Ask for **spouse job-search support** (top companies offer this; even cheap services like Movable.com are useful)

If the company gives a "lump sum" relocation, calculate the actual cost of all of the above. If lump sum < real cost, push for either more cash or specific items covered.

### Multi-offer leverage

The ideal scenario: 2–3 offers in hand. Then you can negotiate honestly:

> "I've got an offer at [Company B] for [TC]. I'd prefer to join you because of [genuine reason]. If you can match within [delta], I'll sign with you tomorrow."

Use this *only* if true. Bluffing here is risky — companies sometimes call.

If you have only one offer but genuine other processes:

> "I've a couple of other interviews in the late stages. Before I commit to a date, I want to see those through. Can you give me [N more days]?"

This is honest, low-key, and usually granted.
