# Part 7 — The first 90 days and beyond

Landing the job is half the work. Staying happy and effective in the first year is the other half. Most failed relocations don't fail at the visa stage — they fail in months 3–6, when the new-job adrenaline wears off and the loneliness, paperwork, and culture shock pile up.

This part is the playbook for not letting that happen.

## Chapter 19. Settling in without burning out

### Week 1 — Survive

Your only goals this week:

- **Don't break anything at work.** Be friendly, ask many questions, write everything down.
- **Sort the four basics:**
  1. Address registration (*Anmeldung* / BSN registration / National Insurance number / SSN application)
  2. Local bank account opened
  3. Permanent SIM card
  4. Health insurance card delivery
- **Find a grocery store, a pharmacy, and a doctor near your apartment.**
- **Sleep.** Jet lag is real. Don't fight it; just sleep when you're tired.

### Week 2–4 — Build infrastructure

- **Public transport pass / bike** — the way locals move. Buy one.
- **Gym, sport, or community.** Pick one before week 4 — it's the antidote to the loneliness wave that hits in week 6.
- **Find a barber/hairdresser, dentist, GP.** You'll need them; building these on the fly during a problem is worse.
- **Set up a savings account or pension contribution.** Many countries have tax-advantaged retirement vehicles (UK ISA, US 401(k), DE betriebliche Altersvorsorge, NL pensioenregeling). Use them from month 1.

### Month 2 — Find your tribe

This is the unsexy but most important advice in the entire book: **make 3 friends in the first 90 days, or you'll go home.**

The data is consistent — international hires who don't build a social network in their first 3 months are 3–5× more likely to leave within a year. The visa was the easy part. The friendships are the hard part.

How:

- **InterNations** events ([internations.org](https://internations.org)) — every major city. Slightly corporate vibe but it works.
- **Meetup.com** — for your specific hobbies and tech stack.
- **Local sports clubs** — the easiest social entry point in NL/DE/CH/AT.
- **Your country's diaspora community** — find an Indian / Brazilian / Nigerian / Filipino / Romanian / Turkish association. Real friendships often start there.
- **Co-working / hot desking 1 day a week** — even if your company is fully remote-friendly. Gets you out.
- **Run clubs / cycling clubs** — every European city has them. Show up Tuesday morning at 7am.
- **Language exchange (Tandem app, in-person Polyglot meetups)** — practice the local language *and* meet people.

### Month 3 — Reflect and recalibrate

By month 3, you've stopped panicking and started having opinions. Schedule a 1-hour solo "performance review of yourself":

- **Work:** how am I doing? What's working? What's a struggle? Is my manager giving useful feedback?
- **Life:** am I making friends? Am I exercising? Am I eating well? Am I drinking too much?
- **Money:** what's my real net pay vs expectation? Am I saving? Are there benefits I haven't claimed?
- **Visa / immigration:** is everything filed correctly? Any deadlines I'm forgetting?
- **Long-term:** is this country / company / role still where I want to be in 3 years?

Adjust accordingly.

### Common emotional patterns by month

- **Month 1:** Honeymoon. Everything is exciting and new.
- **Month 2:** First crash. Loneliness, cultural friction, "what have I done." This is the most dangerous month — many quit here.
- **Month 3–4:** Stabilisation. Either you've started building a real life, or you've decided to leave.
- **Month 6:** Real settling. You start having favourite places.
- **Month 9–12:** Either deeply happy or planning your next move.

The literature on expat adjustment is consistent: **make it past month 4, you usually make it past year 3**. So plan for month 2's slump deliberately — set up support before you need it.

### Cultural adjustment shortcuts

A few country-specific tips that took me a while to learn:

**Germany:** Read the rules. *Hausordnung* (house rules) include things like "no laundry on Sundays" — not a joke, neighbours will report you. Quiet hours (*Ruhezeit*) are 22:00–06:00 and all-day Sunday. Sundays: shops are closed. Plan groceries Saturday.

**Netherlands:** Direct feedback is friendship, not aggression. If a Dutch colleague tells you your code "is not great," they're not insulting you, they're being respectful by being honest.

**Switzerland:** Quietness is sacred. Don't run washing machines after 22:00. Don't flush the toilet between 22:00 and 06:00 in shared houses (yes, really, in some places). Recycle religiously.

**UK:** Pubs are the social infrastructure. Going for "a pint" after work isn't optional in many companies — it's where work relationships are actually built.

**US:** Networking is genuinely important. The casual lunch invite, the "let's grab coffee," the LinkedIn connection — it compounds over years. Say yes more.

### How to tell if you're not doing well

Self-checklist (be honest):

- I haven't called/video-chatted with a friend or family member in >7 days
- I'm using alcohol/screen time as my main coping mechanism
- I'm avoiding work conversations because they take effort
- I'm researching how to "go home" weekly
- I haven't made a single non-colleague friend after month 3

If 2+ of these are true, **act**. Therapy is real and accessible (Talkspace, BetterHelp internationally; local in-network therapists via your insurance). It's not weak. It's expensive to under-handle.

## Chapter 20. The path to permanent residency and citizenship

This is the long game. Most people lose track of it because the day-to-day demands attention, but small actions early dramatically reduce the time to PR.

### Year 1–2: Build the foundation

- **Don't change jobs unnecessarily.** Many visa-to-PR clocks reset on job change. Read the fine print:
  - **DE Blue Card:** clock continues even on job change (within rules).
  - **UK Skilled Worker:** clock continues on job change *if* the new job is also sponsored.
  - **US H-1B → green card:** changing jobs *during PERM/I-140* can reset things; before is fine, after is fine.
  - **NL HSM:** clock continues if you stay an HSM.
  - **CH B-permit:** must remain employed; not formally tied to one employer after first year.
- **Start the local language.** B1 cuts off years from PR in DE (21 vs 27 months), NL (5 vs 7 years for citizenship), AT, CH, etc.
- **Stay tax-compliant.** Years with tax issues are commonly disqualifying.
- **Don't leave the country for >180 days/year.** Most PR clocks pause if you're absent.

### Year 3–5: The PR push

This is when most people qualify. Concrete actions:

- **Pass the language exam early.** Goethe-Institut for German, NT2 for Dutch, Cambridge for English. Cost: €100–€300; saves you stress later.
- **Pass the citizenship/integration test.** Most countries have one (Leben in Deutschland, KNS in NL, Life in the UK).
- **Gather your documents:** continuous tax records, payslips, passport stamps, proof of address.
- **Apply for PR exactly when eligible.** Don't wait. The clock to citizenship usually starts again at PR.

### Year 5–10: Citizenship

Citizenship rules vary widely. Quick map:

- **Germany:** 5 years (3 with extra integration), dual citizenship now allowed since 2024.
- **Netherlands:** 5 years; dual citizenship allowed in some cases (married to Dutch citizen, certain nationalities).
- **UK:** 1 year after ILR (so 6 years total); dual citizenship freely allowed.
- **Ireland:** 5 years out of the previous 9; 1 year continuous immediately before applying. Dual allowed.
- **Switzerland:** 10 years usually; demanding cultural and language tests.
- **Sweden:** 5 years; dual allowed.
- **US:** 5 years on green card (3 if married to US citizen); dual allowed in practice.
- **Canada:** 3 of last 5 years on PR; dual allowed.
- **Australia:** 4 years residence including 1 year as PR; dual allowed.

If you have any chance of wanting EU citizenship long-term, **Ireland and Germany are the fastest predictable paths** for tech workers. Both yield EU passports — the highest-mobility document on earth.

### Why citizenship matters even if you stay loyal

- Voting rights
- No more visa renewals, ever
- Easier travel (US passport: 174 visa-free destinations; German: 188; Swiss: 187)
- Easier to sponsor your parents/siblings
- Some markets (defense, government, banking) only hire citizens for senior roles
- Insurance against your home country's politics changing
- You can live and work anywhere in the EU without further paperwork (huge if you become Irish/German/Dutch)

## A short closing note

I built this book because the people I want to help most are the ones who can't afford a $5,000 immigration consultant or a $300 career coach. If you're reading this, you've already shown the resourcefulness it takes to make this work. The rest is patience, the system, and time.

Three things I want you to take away:

1. **The system in this book works only if you run it.** 200 applications. Tailored. Outreach attached. Over months. Not 20 in a weekend.

2. **Visa sponsorship is not a charity. It's a market.** You're not asking for a favour; you're a candidate offering value. Frame yourself, and your messages, accordingly.

3. **Your first year abroad will be harder than you expect.** Plan for it. Build the social and emotional infrastructure before you need it.
