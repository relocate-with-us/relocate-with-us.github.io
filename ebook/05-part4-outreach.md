# Part 4 — Outreach that gets replies

Most international candidates do exactly one thing: hit "Apply" and wait. Then they're surprised when nothing happens.

The single biggest unlock for this entire book is in this part: **for every job you apply to, also send 1–3 direct messages to humans inside that company**. Recruiter, hiring manager, or someone on the team. This 3× to 5× your interview rate. I'm not exaggerating; I've measured it on my own searches and on dozens of people I've coached.

## Chapter 10. Finding the right humans

You're looking for three types of people:

1. **The recruiter** assigned to this role (or any tech recruiter at the company)
2. **The hiring manager** (the person whose team you'd join, usually a Director, Head of Engineering, or Engineering Manager)
3. **A team member** who can refer you internally

You only need to reach *one* of these. If you can reach all three, even better, but don't spam — pick the most strategic.

### Finding recruiters on LinkedIn

Go to LinkedIn People search. Use this query:

```
("Tech Recruiter" OR "Talent Partner" OR "Talent Acquisition" OR "People Partner")
+ [Company name]
```

Filter by current company. You'll usually see 2–10 recruiters. Pick:

- The one with "Senior" or "Lead" in their title (they often own the senior reqs)
- The one whose past LinkedIn posts mention your role / region (e.g., "We're hiring backend engineers in Amsterdam!")
- The one whose profile mentions your home country / language (warm signal)

If the company posted the job recently, the *poster* of the job is often the recruiter — check the original LinkedIn job post for "Posted by" and message that person.

### Finding hiring managers

Search LinkedIn People for:

```
("Engineering Manager" OR "Director of Engineering" OR "Head of [DOMAIN]" OR "Staff Engineer" OR "Tech Lead")
+ [Company name]
+ [Team / Domain — e.g., "Payments", "Platform", "Infra"]
```

For senior roles, the hiring manager is often listed on the JD directly ("you'll report to ___" or "led by ___"). If not, infer from the team description.

### Finding team members for referrals

Most useful when:

- You went to the same university as someone there (alumni connect)
- You worked at the same previous company as someone there (ex-colleague)
- You contributed to an open-source project they maintain
- You commented thoughtfully on their post in the past month

Tools that help:

- LinkedIn alumni filter (your school's page → Alumni → filter by current company)
- [HirePath](https://hirepath.io) — finds your warm intros
- Slack communities (Locally Optimistic for data, Rands Leadership for eng leaders, kubernetes Slack, etc.)
- Discord communities for your tech stack
- GitHub — find people who have committed to the company's public repos

### A note on LinkedIn premium

Worth it for **2 months only**, during your active search. Reasons:

1. **InMail credits** — you can message anyone, even outside your network (5 credits/month on basic premium)
2. **"Open candidates" filter** in recruiter messages
3. Seeing who viewed your profile (useful signal)

Cancel after you accept an offer.

## Chapter 11. The cold-outreach scripts

These are the templates I've used and the ones I've watched work for hundreds of others. Length matters: under 100 words for LinkedIn DM, under 150 for InMail, under 200 for email. **Asking exactly one question** dramatically increases reply rate.

### Template 1 — The recruiter ping (after applying)

Send this *within 60 minutes* of submitting your application.

> Hi [Name] — just submitted my application for [Role] at [Company]. I'm a [Years]-year [Role] currently at [Company / freelance], based in [Country]. Most relevant background: [one specific achievement that maps to the JD, with metric]. I'm fully eligible for [VISA] and can relocate to [City] within [N] weeks.
> 
> Happy to share any context that's useful — would a 15-min intro chat make sense?
> 
> Cheers, [Name]

Reply rate from people I've coached: 25–40% when sent within an hour of applying.

### Template 2 — The hiring manager ping (cold, no application yet)

Higher-risk, higher-reward. Use when you're confident you're a fit and the JD points clearly at this person's team.

> Hi [Name], saw your team's recent [specific thing — recent launch, talk, paper, hiring post]. I've been working on [related thing] at [Company] for the past [N] years — [one quantified achievement that's directly relevant to their work].
> 
> Considering applying to [Role] on your team. Two quick things: (1) does the team currently sponsor visas / relocation to [City]? (2) is it OK if I mention you when I apply?
> 
> Either way, your work on [thing] is the kind of stuff I want to be doing — wanted to make sure my application gets read carefully.

Reply rate when personalised to a specific recent thing they did: ~30%. When generic: ~5%.

The "is it OK if I mention you when I apply" line is a soft permission play — most people say yes, and now your application has a name attached.

### Template 3 — The internal referral request (warm contact)

When you have an existing connection to someone at the company:

> Hey [Name], hope you've been well. I noticed [Company] is hiring for [Role] — that's the kind of work I've been doing for the last [N] years (most recently [one specific project/achievement]).
> 
> Would you be open to referring me internally? I know the bar is high so completely understand if you'd rather not — happy to send my resume and a tailored summary so you can decide. No hard feelings either way.

Always offer them an out. People dislike feeling pressured to vouch.

If they say yes, send within 30 minutes:

- Resume PDF
- 4-line summary they can paste verbatim into the referral form
- The exact role link
- A single line about why you specifically want this role at this company

Make their task as small as possible.

### Template 4 — The follow-up (5 working days after applying / messaging, no reply)

Send once. *Once*. Then move on.

> Hi [Name], following up on my application/note from [day]. Re: [Role] — I'd love to be considered. If timing's bad, totally understand; if there's anything I can clarify (visa, availability, salary expectations), just say the word.

If no reply to the follow-up, drop it. Don't send a third. Some people are just busy or aren't the right person — neither is a sign you should harass them.

### Template 5 — The post-interview thank-you (within 24 hours)

After every screen / loop / interview:

> Hi [Name], thanks for the conversation today — I particularly enjoyed [specific thing they said or asked]. Wanted to reinforce one point on [topic that came up]: [a 2-line clearer answer / additional context you didn't get to].
> 
> Looking forward to next steps — let me know if I can provide anything else.

The "additional context" trick: pick one question where you wished you'd given a better answer, and give the better answer here. Hiring managers love this — it shows reflection and depth.

### Template 6 — The "haven't heard back, gracefully nudging" message (10 working days after final round)

> Hi [Name], wanted to check in on the [Role] process — last we spoke, I think next steps would be around [date]. I'm continuing to interview elsewhere and have a couple of conversations advancing; before making any decisions, I want to make sure [Company] has what they need from me.
> 
> Happy to wait or to provide anything that would help close it out.

The "interviewing elsewhere" line is honest, low-key competitive pressure. Don't lie if you don't have other processes — but if you do (and you should, you're applying widely), it's fine to mention.

### Template 7 — The recruiter cold (no specific role)

When you really want to work at a company that doesn't have an open role yet:

> Hi [Name], I'm a [Years]-year [Role] specialising in [domain]. I'm not seeing an open role on the team I'd want to join at [Company], but I'd love to be on your radar for when one opens up. Most relevant work: [achievement]. Eligible for [visa]; could move within [N] weeks of an offer.
> 
> Worth a 15-min chat at any point?

10–20% reply rate. Often results in a contact who pings you when something opens.

### Common outreach mistakes that kill replies

I review a lot of cold messages. The pattern of failure:

- **Too long.** Cut by 30%. Then 30% again.
- **Too generic.** "I love your company's mission!" — meaningless. Show you read something specific.
- **No metric.** "I have lots of experience in X" — prove it. Numbers.
- **Multiple questions.** One. Question. Per. Message.
- **Burying the ask.** Lead with context, but make the ask explicit by line 3.
- **Pity framing.** "I'm desperately looking for a job because…" — instant rejection. You're an expert seeking a great team, not a beggar.
- **Asking for "any opportunities."** Specific role. Specific team. Specific reason.
- **Apologising.** "Sorry to bother you" — signals low value. Don't.

### Outreach cadence and volume

If you're seriously job hunting:

- **Per week:** 25 applications + 25 outreach messages (1:1 ratio, sent for the same companies)
- **Reply rate target:** 10–15% of outreach
- **Rejection rate:** ~80%. This is normal. Don't take it personally.
- **Pipeline goal:** 8–15 active processes (applied + at least one back-and-forth with a human) at any given time

A "no reply" is not a "no" until you've followed up once. Then it's a no. Move on.

## Chapter 12. The visa question — when and how to bring it up

This is the single most-Googled question in international tech hiring. My answer is structured by stage:

### Stage 1: Application

**Always state your visa status proactively.** On the resume's top line, in the cover letter, or in any free-text field:

> *Eligible for the EU Blue Card based on my MSc and target salary > €50k. Open to immediate relocation to Berlin, Munich, Amsterdam.*

or

> *Currently authorised to work in [country]; would require H-1B / Skilled Worker sponsorship for [target country].*

Hiding it is the worst strategy. The recruiter will figure it out from your address, your work history, or the screen call — and now they're annoyed.

### Stage 2: Recruiter screen

If you stated it in the application, they may not raise it. If they do, your answer should be calm, prepared, and concise:

> "Yes, I'd need [VISA] sponsorship to work in [country]. I qualify based on my [degree / experience / target salary]. The standard process for this visa takes [N] weeks once an offer is signed, and I've already gathered [degree certificate, references, etc.] in case it speeds things up. Is that something your company supports?"

Three things this answer does:

1. Confirms you understand the process (not a flight risk)
2. Shows you've done the homework (low ops cost for them)
3. Returns the ball — *they* now have to commit

If they say no, accept it gracefully and ask if they'd consider a remote contract instead. If they say yes, the rest of the screen is normal.

### Stage 3: Onsite / loop

By this stage, the visa question is settled. If it comes up again, you're in a re-confirmation conversation — keep your answer the same as Stage 2. The danger zone is interviewers who weren't briefed properly and ask "wait, do you actually live here?" Don't get rattled. Repeat your line.

### Stage 4: Offer / negotiation

Two practical points to negotiate:

1. **Who pays for the visa?** Defaults vary. Push for the company to cover application fees, legal fees, and (UK) Immigration Skills Charge. Most reputable companies cover all three.
2. **Relocation package.** Standard packages range from €2,500 (lump sum) to €15,000 (full move + flights + temporary accommodation + tax assistance). Push for whichever is more useful to your situation.

Specific negotiables to ask for if not offered:
- Flight for you and partner (1 way + 1 visit before move)
- Temporary accommodation for 30–60 days
- Shipping container (if moving with furniture)
- Visa lawyer fees
- Tax filing assistance for the first year (you'll appreciate this)
- 30% ruling application support (NL specific)
- Spouse job-search support (real benefit at top firms)

If the company can't move on base salary, *push hard* on these — they're often easier to grant than salary, and worth a lot.

### Common visa-question mistakes

- **Apologising for needing a visa.** "I'm so sorry but I'd need…" — don't. You're providing value; they're paying for that.
- **Being vague about the visa type.** "Some kind of work visa" — sounds amateur. Know the name.
- **Promising things you can't.** "I can be there in 2 weeks!" if your visa actually takes 8 — disappoints later.
- **Bringing up family logistics too early.** Wait until after the offer. They don't need to hear about your dog yet.
- **Negotiating relocation before the offer.** Fight base salary first. Relocation second.
